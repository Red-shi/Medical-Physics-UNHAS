Anatomy, function, and the process of converting food into usable energy.

The Digestive System is the organ system responsible for breaking down food, absorbing nutrients, and eliminating waste. It consists of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, or alimentary canal (a continuous tube from mouth to anus), and several accessory digestive organs.

1. Primary Functions of Digestion

  • Ingestion: Taking food into the mouth.
  • Secretion: Release of water, acids, buffers, and enzymes into the GI tract.
  • Mixing and Propulsion: Alternating contraction and relaxation of smooth muscles in the GI tract (known as peristalsis) to propel food along the canal.
  • Digestion: The mechanical and chemical breakdown of food into small molecules.
  • Absorption: Passage of digested nutrients from the GI tract into the blood or lymph.
  • Defecation: Elimination of non-digestible substances from the body.

2. The Gastrointestinal (GI) Tract

A. Upper GI Tract

  • Mouth (Oral Cavity): Site of mechanical digestion (chewing) and the start of chemical digestion (salivary amylase breaks down starch).
  • Pharynx and Esophagus: Passageways for food. The epiglottis prevents food from entering the trachea. The esophagus uses peristalsis to move the food bolus to the stomach.
  • Stomach: A J-shaped organ that mixes food with gastric juice (containing HCl and Pepsin). HCl kills microbes and activates pepsin for protein digestion. The resulting mixture is called chyme.

B. Lower GI Tract

  • Small Intestine: The major site of chemical digestion and absorption. It is divided into three sections: Duodenum, Jejunum, and Ileum. Its inner wall is lined with villi and microvilli (creating the "brush border") to maximize surface area for absorption.
  • Large Intestine: Responsible for the completion of absorption (mainly water and electrolytes), formation of feces, and elimination. It contains specialized bacteria that synthesize some vitamins (like Vitamin K).
  • Rectum and Anus: Storage and elimination of feces.

3. Accessory Digestive Organs

These organs aid in digestion by producing or storing secretions that flow into the GI tract.

A. Liver and Gallbladder

  • Liver: Produces bile, an emulsifying agent necessary for lipid digestion. It also processes nutrients absorbed from the small intestine via the hepatic portal system.
  • Gallbladder: Stores and concentrates bile until it is needed in the small intestine (duodenum).

B. Pancreas

A gland that produces crucial digestive enzymes and hormones.

  • Exocrine Function: Produces pancreatic juice containing Amylase (starch digestion), Lipase (fat digestion), and Trypsin (protein digestion). Pancreatic juice also contains bicarbonate to neutralize the acidic chyme coming from the stomach.
  • Endocrine Function: Produces hormones like insulin and glucagon (part of the Endocrine System, controlling blood sugar).
