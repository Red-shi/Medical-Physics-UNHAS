A structural hierarchy from the smallest chemical units to the complete organism.

The human body is organized into a highly complex, hierarchical structure, where each level builds upon the previous one. Understanding this organization is fundamental to anatomy, physiology, and medical physics, as radiation and disease affect these levels differently.

The Six Levels of Structural Organization

1. Chemical Level

This is the most basic level, dealing with the structure of atoms and their interactions to form molecules.

  • Atoms: The smallest units of matter (e.g., Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Carbon (C), Nitrogen (N)). In medical physics, the composition of tissues (effective atomic number) is crucial for X-ray interaction.
  • Molecules: Two or more atoms joined together (e.g., Water (H2O), Glucose, Amino Acids).
  • Macromolecules: Large, complex biological molecules (e.g., proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, and DNA). DNA is the primary target for radiation-induced damage.

2. Cellular Level

Molecules combine to form organelles, which are the specialized structures within cells. The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of the human body.

  • Examples: Muscle cells (myocytes), nerve cells (neurons), and blood cells (erythrocytes, leukocytes).
  • Key Concept: Cells are the first living level where complex biological functions (metabolism, reproduction, and response to stimuli) occur.

3. Tissue Level

A tissue is a group of similar cells that work together to perform a specific function.

  • Epithelial Tissue: Covers body surfaces and lines hollow organs (e.g., skin, lining of digestive tract).
  • Connective Tissue: Supports, protects, and binds organs together (e.g., bone, cartilage, blood, fat).
  • Muscular Tissue: Produces movement (e.g., skeletal, cardiac, smooth).
  • Nervous Tissue: Carries information via nerve impulses (e.g., brain, spinal cord, nerves).

4. Organ Level

An organ consists of two or more different types of tissues that work together to perform complex, specialized physiological functions.

  • Examples: The heart (made of cardiac muscle, nervous, and connective tissue), lungs, liver, brain, and stomach.
  • Relevance to Protection: In radiation protection, organs have specific Tissue Weighting Factors (W T) based on their sensitivity to radiation-induced cancer.

5. Organ System Level

An organ system consists of related organs that have a common function.

  • Skeletal System: Provides support and protection (bones, cartilage).
  • Cardiovascular System: Circulates blood (heart, blood vessels).
  • Respiratory System: Moves air in and out (lungs, trachea).
  • Nervous System: Coordinates bodily activities (brain, spinal cord, nerves).

6. Organismal Level

This is the highest level of organization. It represents the complete living individual.

  • Definition: All the organ systems of the body work together cooperatively to maintain life, health, and homeostasis.

Key Concept: Homeostasis

The ability of the human body to maintain a relatively stable internal environment despite changes in the external environment. All levels of organization, particularly the organ systems, function to maintain this balance. Disruptions to cellular function (e.g., due to disease or radiation damage) threaten homeostasis.
