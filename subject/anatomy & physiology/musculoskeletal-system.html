Structure, function, and components of the body's framework and movement system.

The Musculoskeletal System is an organ system that provides the body with form, stability, and the ability to move. It is composed of two major interacting components: the Skeletal System (bones and cartilage) and the Muscular System (skeletal muscles).

1. Functions of the Musculoskeletal System

  • Support: Provides a framework that supports the body's soft tissues and maintains the body's shape.
  • Movement: Muscles pull on bones, acting as levers, to facilitate locomotion and movement of body parts.
  • Protection: The skeleton protects vital internal organs (e.g., the skull protects the brain; the rib cage protects the heart and lungs).
  • Mineral Homeostasis: Bones store several minerals, especially calcium and phosphorus, releasing them into the blood when needed.
  • Blood Cell Production: Red bone marrow within certain bones is the site of hematopoiesis (formation of blood cells).

2. Components of the Skeletal System

The skeleton is composed of bone and cartilage, and provides structural support.

A. Bone (Osseous Tissue)

  • Composition: A hard, dense connective tissue primarily composed of an extracellular matrix of collagen fibers and crystallized mineral salts, mainly calcium phosphate (Ca3(PO4)2).
  • Types of Bone Tissue:
    • Compact Bone: Dense, found on the exterior of bones. Provides protection and support.
    • Spongy (Cancellous) Bone: Porous, found inside the ends of long bones and in vertebrae. Contains red bone marrow.
  • Relevance to Imaging: Bone's high density and high effective atomic number (Z) cause it to attenuate X-rays strongly, making it appear radiopaque (white) in conventional radiography.

B. Joints (Articulations)

Points where two or more bones meet. Classified structurally (fibrous, cartilaginous, synovial) and functionally (synarthrosis, amphiarthrosis, diarthrosis).

  • Synovial Joints: Most common and moveable joints (e.g., knee, shoulder). They contain a fluid-filled cavity protected by articular cartilage.
  • Ligaments: Connect bone to bone, providing stability and limiting excessive movement.
  • Tendons: Connect muscle to bone, transmitting the force of muscle contraction to the bone to cause movement.

3. Components of the Muscular System

The muscular system is responsible for generating movement and maintaining posture.

Skeletal Muscle

  • Function: Primarily responsible for voluntary movements (locomotion, posture). These muscles attach to bones, usually by tendons.
  • Structure: Composed of long, cylindrical muscle fibers that contain specialized contractile proteins, Actin and Myosin.
  • Mechanism of Contraction: Muscle contraction is triggered by a nerve impulse, which leads to the sliding of actin filaments over myosin filaments (the Sliding Filament Theory), shortening the muscle fiber.

Other Muscle Types

  • Cardiac Muscle: Found only in the heart wall; involuntary and striated. Responsible for pumping blood.
  • Smooth Muscle: Found in the walls of hollow organs (e.g., digestive tract, blood vessels); involuntary and non-striated. Responsible for processes like peristalsis.
