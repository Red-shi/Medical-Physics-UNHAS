Integration, control, and communication networks of the body.

The Nervous System is the body's primary control and communication system. It is responsible for rapidly sensing changes in the internal and external environments, processing this information, and initiating a motor response. The functional unit of this system is the neuron.

1. Organization of the Nervous System

A. Central Nervous System (CNS)

The processing center of the body. It integrates incoming sensory information and dictates outgoing motor responses.

  • Brain: The primary control center. Responsible for complex functions like consciousness, memory, and voluntary movement. Major parts include the Cerebrum, Cerebellum, and Brainstem.
  • Spinal Cord: Connects the brain to the rest of the body. It mediates most reflex activities.

B. Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

All nervous tissue outside the CNS. It acts as the communication link, relaying signals to and from the CNS.

  • Afferent (Sensory) Division: Carries impulses TO the CNS from sensory receptors.
  • Efferent (Motor) Division: Carries impulses FROM the CNS to effectors (muscles and glands).

2. Functional Divisions of the PNS

The efferent division is further split based on the type of effector tissue:

Somatic Nervous System (SNS)

Controls voluntary movement by stimulating skeletal muscles.

Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)

Controls involuntary activities by stimulating smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands. It has two main branches that act antagonistically:

  • Sympathetic Division: "Fight-or-Flight" response. Prepares the body for high energy expenditure (e.g., increased heart rate, dilated pupils).
  • Parasympathetic Division: "Rest-and-Digest" response. Conserves and restores body energy (e.g., decreased heart rate, increased digestive activity).

3. Physiology of Nervous Signaling

The Neuron and the Action Potential

  • Neuron: Specialized cell that generates and transmits electrical signals. Communication occurs through rapid changes in the membrane potential.
  • Action Potential: A rapid, transient, all-or-none electrical signal that travels along the axon, generated by the opening and closing of voltage-gated ion channels (primarily Na + and K +).
  • Synapse: The junction between two neurons or between a neuron and an effector cell. Signals are transmitted across the synapse primarily via neurotransmitters (chemical messengers).

4. The Senses (Sensory Receptors)

The senses are specialized parts of the nervous system that allow the body to perceive its environment.

A. General Senses (Somatic and Visceral)

Receptors scattered throughout the body (skin, organs, muscles, joints).

  • Touch and Pressure: Mechanoreceptors in the skin.
  • Temperature: Thermoreceptors.
  • Pain: Nociceptors (pain receptors), crucial for protection.
  • Proprioception: Sense of body position and movement, located in muscles, tendons, and joints.

B. Special Senses

Receptors concentrated in complex sense organs in the head.

  • Smell (Olfaction): Chemoreceptors in the nasal cavity.
  • Taste (Gustation): Chemoreceptors in taste buds on the tongue.
  • Sight (Vision): Photoreceptors (Rods and Cones) in the retina of the eye.
  • Hearing (Audition): Mechanoreceptors (hair cells) in the cochlea of the inner ear, sensitive to sound vibrations.
  • Balance (Equilibrium): Mechanoreceptors in the semicircular canals and vestibule of the inner ear, sensitive to gravity and motion.
