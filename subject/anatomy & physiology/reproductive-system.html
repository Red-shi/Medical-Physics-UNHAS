Anatomy, physiology, and the creation of new life.

The Reproductive System is the only organ system not essential for the survival of the individual but is essential for the survival of the species. Its primary function is to produce offspring. This system relies heavily on the Endocrine System for control and regulation.

1. The Male Reproductive System

A. Primary Sex Organs (Gonads)

  • Testes: Produce male gametes (sperm) and secrete male sex hormones, primarily Testosterone. Sperm production (spermatogenesis) occurs in the seminiferous tubules.

B. Ducts and Accessory Glands

  • Epididymis: Site of sperm maturation and storage.
  • Ductus (Vas) Deferens: Transports sperm from the epididymis to the ejaculatory duct.
  • Seminal Vesicles: Produce seminal fluid (60% of semen volume), which contains fructose for sperm energy.
  • Prostate Gland: Produces fluid that activates sperm and aids motility.

2. The Female Reproductive System

A. Primary Sex Organs (Gonads)

  • Ovaries: Produce female gametes (oocytes/ova) and secrete female sex hormones, Estrogen and Progesterone. Oocyte production (oogenesis) occurs here.

B. Accessory Structures

  • Uterine (Fallopian) Tubes: Transport the ovum from the ovary to the uterus. Site where fertilization typically occurs.
  • Uterus: Site of fetal development. The inner lining, the endometrium, is shed during menstruation if pregnancy does not occur.
  • Vagina: Passageway for birth, menstrual flow, and intercourse.

3. Hormonal Control and Gametogenesis

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) Axis

Both male and female reproductive cycles are controlled by the HPG axis:

  1. Hypothalamus secretes GnRH (Gonadotropin-releasing hormone).
  2. GnRH stimulates the Anterior Pituitary to release FSH (Follicle-stimulating hormone) and LH (Luteinizing hormone).
  3. FSH and LH target the gonads (testes/ovaries) to stimulate gamete production and hormone secretion (Testosterone, Estrogen, Progesterone).

Gametogenesis (Gamete Formation)

  • Spermatogenesis: Continuous process in the testes starting at puberty, resulting in four viable sperm per precursor cell.
  • Oogenesis Process starts before birth, pauses, and resumes at puberty. Typically results in only one viable ovum and two polar bodies per cycle.

4. Special Considerations for Medical Physics

The reproductive organs, particularly the gonads, are highly sensitive to radiation due to the presence of rapidly dividing or differentiating germ cells.

  • Radiation Protection: The gonads are assigned a high Tissue Weighting Factor (W T) by the ICRP, emphasizing the need for robust shielding during imaging procedures to protect against genetic (hereditary) effects.
  • bImaging: Procedures like mammography, pelvic ultrasound, and specialized MRI sequences are crucial for diagnosing reproductive health issues and monitoring pregnancy.
