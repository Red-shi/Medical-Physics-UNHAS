Structure and function of the body's gas exchange mechanism.

The Respiratory System is responsible for the crucial process of respiration‚Äîthe exchange of oxygen (O2) and carbon dioxide (CO2) between the atmosphere, the blood, and the body‚Äôs cells. This system can be structurally divided into the Upper and Lower respiratory tracts, and functionally into the Conducting and Respiratory zones.

1. Primary Functions of Respiration

  • Pulmonary Ventilation (Breathing): Movement of air into (inspiration) and out of (expiration) the lungs.
  • External Respiration: Exchange of gases between the air in the lungs (alveoli) and the blood in the pulmonary capillaries.
  • Internal Respiration: Exchange of gases between the blood in systemic capillaries and the tissue cells.
  • Other Functions: Producing sound (vocalization), filtering inhaled air, and regulating blood pH.

2. Structural Organization

A. The Conducting Zone (Air Passageway)

A series of interconnecting cavities and tubes that filter, warm, and moisten air before it reaches the lungs. No gas exchange occurs here.

  • Nose and Nasal Cavity: Warms, moistens, and filters air; contains olfactory receptors.
  • Pharynx (Throat): Passageway for food and air. Divided into nasopharynx, oropharynx, and laryngopharynx.
  • Larynx (Voice Box): Contains the vocal cords; connects the pharynx to the trachea. The epiglottis covers the larynx during swallowing.
  • Trachea (Windpipe): A tube lined with C-shaped cartilage rings for support; extends to the chest cavity.
  • Bronchial Tree: The trachea divides into the primary, secondary, and tertiary bronchi, leading into smaller bronchioles.

B. The Respiratory Zone (Gas Exchange Site)

The site where the main function of the respiratory system takes place.

  • Respiratory Bronchioles: Terminal branches that begin the exchange zone.
  • Alveolar Ducts: Tubes leading from the bronchioles.
  • Alveoli (Air Sacs): Tiny, grape-like sacs that form the primary surface for gas exchange. The walls are one cell thick, surrounded by a dense network of pulmonary capillaries.
  • Respiratory Membrane: The thin barrier (alveolar wall + capillary wall) across which O2 and CO2 diffuse.

3. Mechanics of Breathing and Gas Exchange

A. Ventilation (Inspiration and Expiration)

  • Inspiration (Inhalation): The diaphragm contracts (moves down), and the external intercostal muscles contract (pulling the ribs up and out). This increases the volume of the chest cavity, decreasing internal lung pressure (Boyle's Law), causing air to rush in.
  • Expiration (Exhalation): The diaphragm and external intercostals relax, decreasing the chest cavity volume, increasing internal lung pressure, and forcing air out.

B. Gas Exchange (Diffusion)

Gas exchange across the respiratory membrane is governed by Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures and Henry's Law.

  • Partial Pressure: Each gas in a mixture exerts its own pressure independently. Gases diffuse from an area of higher partial pressure to an area of lower partial pressure.
  • External Respiration: O2 partial pressure is high in the alveoli and low in the pulmonary blood, so O2 moves into the blood. CO2 partial pressure is high in the blood and low in the alveoli, so CO2 moves out of the blood.
  • Internal Respiration: O2 partial pressure is high in systemic blood and low in the tissues, so O2 moves into the tissues. CO2 partial pressure is high in the tissues (waste product) and low in the blood, so CO2 moves into the blood.
