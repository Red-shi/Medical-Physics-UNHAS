1. Anatomy: The Study of Structure

Anatomy (from Greek ana meaning "apart" and tome meaning "to cut") is the study of the structure of body parts and their relationships to one another. Essentially, it deals with the name, location, and appearance of body components.

A. Gross (Macroscopic) Anatomy

The study of large body structures, easily visible to the naked eye.

  • Regional Anatomy: All structures (muscles, bones, blood vessels, nerves) in a particular region of the body (e.g., the abdomen or leg) are examined at the same time.
  • Systemic Anatomy: Body structure is studied system by system (e.g., studying the cardiovascular system throughout the entire body).
  • Surface Anatomy: The study of internal structures as they relate to the overlying skin surface.

B. Microscopic Anatomy

The study of structures too small to be seen with the naked eye.

  • Cytology: The study of cells.
  • Histology: The study of tissues.

2. Physiology: The Study of Function

Physiology (from Greek physio meaning "nature" and logia meaning "study of") is the study of the function of the body's structural components. It explains how the body parts work and carry out their life-sustaining activities.

A. Focus and Subdivisions

  • Focus: Physiology focuses on events at the cellular or molecular level, often dealing with chemical and physical principles.
  • Subdivisions: Subdivisions are based on organ systems.
    • Renal Physiology: Concerns kidney function and urine production.
    • Neurophysiology: Explains the workings of the nervous system.
    • Cardiovascular Physiology: Examines the operation of the heart and blood vessels.

3. The Principle of Complementarity of Structure and Function

Anatomy and Physiology are inseparable. What a structure can do depends on its specific form.

"Function always reflects structure."

  • Example: The thin, delicate walls of the lung air sacs (alveoli - structure) allow for rapid, efficient gas exchange (function).
  • Example: The strong, multi-layered walls of the stomach (structure) are built to contain and churn acidic contents (function).

4. Levels of Structural Organization

The human body's structure can be studied on a hierarchy of levels, from the smallest chemical building blocks to the entire organism.

  1. Chemical Level

    Atoms (like C, H, O, N) combine to form molecules (like proteins, carbohydrates, and DNA).

  2. Cellular Level

    The smallest units of living matter. Molecules combine to form organelles, which are the specialized functional parts of a cell (e.g., mitochondria, nucleus).

  3. Groups of similar cells that have a common function. The four basic tissue types are Epithelial, Muscle, Connective, and Nervous tissue.

  4. Organ Level

    A structure composed of two or more different tissue types that performs a specific, often complex, function for the body (e.g., the liver, stomach, or heart).

  5. Organ System Level

    Organs that work together to accomplish a common purpose (e.g., the Digestive System).

  6. Organismal Level

    The sum total of all structural levels working in harmony to keep the organism (the human being) alive.
