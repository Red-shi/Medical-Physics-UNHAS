Identifying molecules by their vibrational fingerprint.

Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy is a powerful analytical technique used to identify functional groups and determine the chemical structure of organic and inorganic molecules. It measures the absorption of infrared radiation by a sample, which causes the molecule's bonds to vibrate.

1. Fundamental Principles and Molecular Vibrations

A. The Infrared Region

  • Spectral Range: Typically covers 4000 cm -1 to 400 cm -1 (measured in wavenumbersâ€”the reciprocal of wavelength, 1/λ). This corresponds to the energy required to excite molecular vibrations.
  • Mechanism: When the frequency of the incident IR radiation exactly matches the natural vibrational frequency of a molecule's bond, energy is absorbed, causing the vibrational amplitude to increase.

B. Types of Vibrations

Molecular bonds can vibrate in specific, quantized modes:

  • 1. Stretching: A change in the bond length along the axis between the two atoms.
    • Symmetric Stretching: Both bonds lengthen or shorten simultaneously.
    • Asymmetric Stretching: One bond lengthens while the other shortens.
  • 2. Bending (Deformation): A change in the angle between two bonds.
    • Scissoring: Two bonds move toward and away from each other.
    • Rocking: Both bonds move back and forth in the same plane.
    • Wagging: Both bonds move back and forth out of the plane.
    • Twisting: One bond moves forward out of the plane while the other moves backward.

2. Selection Rules and Dipole Moment

IR Activity: The Dipole Moment Rule

For a vibrational mode to be IR active (i.e., able to absorb IR light), the vibration must result in a change in the net molecular dipole moment (μ).

  • IR Active: Bonds with inherent polarity (like C=O or O-H) or asymmetrical vibrations are strong IR absorbers.
  • IR Inactive: Highly symmetrical bonds (like the stretching of O=O atau N≡N) do not change the net dipole moment and therefore show no IR signal.

The Fingerprint Region

  • Region: Below ≈ 1500 cm-1.
  • Significance: This region contains complex bending and stretching patterns unique to the entire molecule. It is used for definitive compound identification by matching the spectrum against a library (the molecule's "fingerprint").
  • Functional Group Region: Above ≈ 1500 cm-1. Contains characteristic signals for specific functional groups (e.g., C=O absorbs sharply around 1700 cm -1).

3. Instrumentation (FTIR Spectrometer)

Modern IR instruments primarily use Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) technology, which offers much faster scanning and higher signal-to-noise ratio than older dispersive models.

  • 1. Light Source: A stable, wide-band ceramic source (e.g., Globar or Nernst glower) that emits radiation across the mid-IR range.
  • 2. Interferometer (Michelson Interferometer): The core of the FTIR. It splits the source beam, combines the two paths after one has been reflected by a moving mirror, and generates an interferogram (a signal containing all frequencies encoded simultaneously).
  • 3. Sample Holder: IR beams are absorbed by glass, so the sample is held between polished salt plates (NaCl or KBr) or analyzed using Attenuated Total Reflectance (ATR) accessories.
  • 4. Detector: A highly sensitive detector (e.g., Deuterated Triglycine Sulfate, DTGS, or Mercury Cadmium Telluride, MCT) measures the total interference signal.
  • 5. Computer & FT: The computer uses a mathematical process called the Fourier Transform to decode the interferogram into the conventional spectrum (intensity vs. wavenumber).

4. Applications in Medical and Chemical Fields

  • Quality Control: Verifying the chemical identity and purity of raw materials and synthesized products (e.g., in pharmaceuticals).
  • Polymer Science: Identifying different plastic types and analyzing additives.
  • Forensics: Identifying unknown substances like illicit drugs, fibers, or paints.
  • Biomedical Imaging (FTIR Microscopy): Combining FTIR with microscopy to obtain biochemical maps of tissues and cells for cancer diagnostics, based on differences in protein and lipid content.
