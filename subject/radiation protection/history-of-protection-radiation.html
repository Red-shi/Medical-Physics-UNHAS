From early discoveries to modern safety standards and dose limits.

The history of Radiation Protection is a narrative of scientific discovery, initial enthusiasm, tragic realization of biological harm, and subsequent establishment of robust, physics-based safety standards. The core objective is to prevent deterministic effects (tissue damage) and reduce the probability of stochastic effects (cancer/genetic defects).

3. Modern Dose Limits and the ALARA Principle (1950â€“Present)

The study of Atomic Bomb survivors and radiation workers led to a shift from "tolerance dose" (no deterministic effects) to a focus on preventing stochastic effects.

The ICRP System of Dose Limitation

  • Justification: Any practice involving radiation exposure must yield a net benefit to the exposed individual or society.
  • Optimization (ALARA): All exposures should be kept As Low As Reasonably Achievable, taking economic and social factors into account. This principle is the cornerstone of clinical medical physics safety.
  • Dose Limitation: Strict limits on individual doses (effective dose, equivalent dose) are set for workers and the public to prevent deterministic effects and minimize the probability of stochastic effects.

Key Modern Physics Concepts

Equivalent Dose (HT): Accounts for the biological harm caused by different types of radiation (e.g., photons vs. neutrons) using a Radiation Weighting Factor (W R). Unit: Sievert (Sv).

HT = ΣR WR · DT,R

Effective Dose (E): Accounts for the varying sensitivity of different organs and tissues using a Tissue Weighting Factor (W T). This allows a single value to represent the overall stochastic risk to the entire body. Unit: Sievert (Sv).

E = ΣT WT · HT

  • Patient Protection: The focus in medical physics shifted from solely protecting the radiation worker to meticulous patient dose management, especially with high-dose procedures like CT and Interventional Radiology.

The field of radiation protection continues to evolve, constantly refining dose limits and monitoring techniques based on updated radiobiological data and technological advancements, all guided by the fundamental principles laid out over the last century.
