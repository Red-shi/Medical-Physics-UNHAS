Radiobiology is the interdisciplinary study of the action of ionizing radiation on living things. It encompasses physics (radiation delivery), chemistry (molecular damage), and biology (cellular and tissue response). Its primary goal is to optimize the therapeutic ratio in radiotherapy by maximizing tumor kill while minimizing damage to healthy tissue.

1. The Mechanisms of Initial Damage

A. The Target Theory

  • Concept: The theory posits that for a cell to die, a specific sensitive volume—the target (primarily the DNA in the nucleus)—must be hit and inactivated by the radiation.
  • Direct Action: The radiation (or secondary charged particle) interacts immediately with the DNA molecule, causing breaks or damage. Dominant for High LET radiation.
  • Indirect Action: The radiation interacts with surrounding water (H2O), creating highly reactive free radicals (·OH), which then diffuse and chemically attack the DNA. Dominant for Low LETradiation (X-rays, γ-rays).

2. Cellular Radiosensitivity and Survival

The radiosensitivity of a cell describes its susceptibility to being killed by radiation. This varies significantly based on its properties.

A. The Law of Bergonié and Tribondeau (1906)

This law summarizes the factors that increase radiosensitivity:

  1. Cells that have a high mitotic rate (divide rapidly).
  2. Cells that are undifferentiated (non-specialized).
  3. Cells that have a long future of proliferation (stem cells).

Highly radiosensitive tissues include bone marrow and lymphoid tissue. Radioresistant tissues include nerve and muscle tissue.

B. Cell Survival Curves

These curves graphically illustrate the relationship between the applied radiation dose and the fraction of cells that survive.

  • Shoulder Region (Low Dose): Indicates the cell's ability to accumulate and repair sub-lethal damage.
  • Linear Region (High Dose): Survival decreases exponentially. Cell death often results from single-hit, irreparable damage.

3. Modifying Factors of Radiation Response

Several factors can chemically or physically alter the biological effectiveness of a given dose.

  • Oxygen Enhancement Ratio (OER): The ratio of the dose needed to achieve a certain biological effect under hypoxic conditions versus the dose needed under oxygenated conditions. OER is typically 2 to 3 for low LET radiation, highlighting the oxygen effect (chemical fixation of damage).
  • Relative Biological Effectiveness (RBE): The ratio of the dose of reference radiation (X-rays) to the dose of the test radiation required to produce the same biological effect. RBE is higher for High LET radiation (α, neutrons, protons) because their dense energy deposition is less repairable.
  • Dose Rate: The speed at which the dose is delivered. Lower dose rates allow more time for cellular repair and are thus less effective (less cell kill) than high dose rates for the same total dose.

4. Clinical Applications: The 4 R's of Radiobiology

In Fractionated Radiotherapy (delivering the total dose in small daily fractions), the following processes govern the outcome:

  • 1. Repair: Repair of sublethal damage in normal tissue between fractions (protects normal tissue).
  • 2. Redistribution: Movement of surviving tumor cells into the radiosensitive G2/M phases (increases tumor kill).
  • 3. Repopulation: Cell division in both tumor and normal tissue during the overall treatment time.
  • 4. Reoxygenation: Re-establishment of oxygen supply in the tumor, making previously hypoxic tumor cells more radiosensitive (increases tumor kill).

Therapeutic Ratio

The goal of modern radiotherapy is to find the optimal balance between the probability of tumor control and the probability of normal tissue complications. This is defined by the Therapeutic Ratio, which is ideally maximized by exploiting the biological differences between tumor cells and normal cells (e.g., maximizing the benefits of repair in normal tissue and redistribution/reoxygenation in tumors).
