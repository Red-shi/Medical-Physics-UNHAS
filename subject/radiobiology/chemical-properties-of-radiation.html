The chemical properties of radiation primarily refer to the molecular changes and chemical reactions initiated by the deposition of ionizing radiation energy. In biological systems, these effects are classified into direct and indirect actions, with the indirect actionâ€”the radiolysis of waterâ€”being the dominant pathway for damage.

1. The Two Actions of Radiation Damage

A. Direct Action

  • Mechanism: The primary particle (photon, electron, etc.) or its secondary charged particle interacts directly with a critical biomolecule (like DNA, protein, or lipid) and causes ionization or excitation.
  • Result: This leads to a molecular lesion, such as a single-strand break (SSB) or double-strand break (DSB) in the DNA backbone, rendering the molecule biologically inactive or altered.
  • Relevance: Dominant mechanism for High LET radiation (e.g., alpha particles, neutrons) and for large molecular structures when water content is low.

B. Indirect Action (Dominant in Biological Tissue)

  • Mechanism: The radiation interacts first with the surrounding water (which constitutes about 80% of a cell's mass), producing highly reactive chemical species. These reactive species then diffuse and cause damage to the critical biomolecules.
  • Relevance: Dominant mechanism for Low LET radiation (e.g., X-rays, γ-rays, electrons).

2. Radiolysis of Water (H 20)

Radiolysis of water is the primary event of indirect action. The high energy of ionizing radiation breaks the covalent bonds in water molecules, yielding free radicals and stable products within picoseconds.

A. Primary Products

The overall radiolysis reaction can be simplified as:

H2O → ·OH + e-aq + ·H + H3O+ + H2O2 + H2
  • Hydroxyl Radical (·OH): The most damaging species. It is highly reactive, uncharged, and a powerful oxidizing agent.
  • Hydrated Electron (e-aq): A free electron surrounded by water molecules. It is a powerful reducing agent.
  • Hydrogen Radical (·H):A reactive neutral species.

B. Free Radical Damage

The free radicals, particularly ·OH, rapidly diffuse through the cell and attack organic molecules, initiating chain reactions. For DNA, this attack leads to base damage, sugar damage, and ultimately, strand breaks.

3. The Oxygen Effect (Chemical Sensitization)

The presence of molecular oxygen (O2) significantly increases the biological effectiveness of Low LOW radiation. This enhancement is known as the Oxygen Enhancement Ratio (OER).

The Chemical Fixation Hypothesis

  • Hypoxic (Low O2) State: If a radiation-damaged DNA molecule is without O2, the damage can often be repaired quickly by cellular reducing agents.
  • Oxygenated State: O2 reacts rapidly with DNA radicals, creating peroxy radicals (e.g., DNA-O2·). This reaction "fixes" the damage chemically, making it permanent and much harder for the cell's enzymes to repair.

This is a major challenge in radiation therapy, as many solid tumors have highly hypoxic cores, making them resistant to conventional photon (Low LET) radiotherapy.

4. Chemical Protection and Sensitization

Certain chemicals can modify the effects of radiation, altering the chemical environment before the damage to the DNA is fixed.

  • Radioprotectors: Chemicals that reduce the damage caused by radiation. They typically contain sulfhydryl groups (SH) and act by scavenging free radicals (·OH) before they reach the DNA. (Example: Amifostine).
  • Radiosensitizers: Chemicals that increase the radiation damage, often by mimicking oxygen (hypoxic cell sensitizers) or by interfering with DNA repair mechanisms. (Example: Chemotherapy drugs, or hypoxic sensitizers like Nimorazole).
