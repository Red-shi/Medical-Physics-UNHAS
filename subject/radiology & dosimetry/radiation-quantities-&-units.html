The system for measuring radiation exposure, dose, and biological effect.

Radiation Quantities and Units are essential in Medical Physics and Health Physics for defining the amount of radiation emitted, the energy absorbed by a medium, and the resulting biological risk. The modern system utilizes SI (Syst√®me International) units, though older conventional units are still sometimes encountered.

1. Source Quantity: Activity (Rate of Decay)

Activity measures the rate at which a quantity of radioactive material undergoes spontaneous nuclear decay (disintegration).

Activity (A)

  • Definition: The number of nuclear transformations (disintegrations) occurring per unit time.
  • SI Unit: Becquerel (Bq) 1 Bq = 1 disintegration per second (s -1)
  • Conventional Unit: Curie (Ci) 1 Ci = 3.7 × 1010 Bq (3.7 × 1010 is the approximate activity of 1 gram of Radium-226.)

2. Exposure and Dose in Medium

A. Exposure (X)

A quantity defined only for X and γ rays in air. It measures the ionization produced in a specified mass of air.

  • Definition: The charge (Q) produced by X or γ rays in a mass (m) of air.
  • SI Unit: Coulomb per kilogram (C/kg).
  • Conventional Unit: Roentgen (R) (1 R ≈ 2.58 × 10 -4 C/kg).

B. Absorbed Dose (D)

The most fundamental physical quantity in dosimetry. It measures the energy deposited per unit mass of any material (tissue, bone, water, air).

Absorbed Dose (D)

  • Definition: The energy (E) absorbed per unit mass (m) of the absorbing medium. D = dE/dm
  • SI Unit: Gray (Gy) 1 Gy = 1 Joule per kilogram (1 J/k)
  • Conventional Unit: Rad (Radiation Absorbed Dose) 1 Gy = 100 Rad

3. Biological Quantities (Risk and Effect)

Since equal absorbed doses of different types of radiation (e.g., α vs. γ) cause different amounts of biological damage due to varying LET, the following quantities are used to standardize the risk.

A. Equivalent Dose (H)

Accounts for the varying biological effectiveness of different radiation types.

H = D × W R
  • Definition: Absorbed dose (D) multiplied by the Radiation Weighting Factor (WR), which is based on the radiation type. (WR = 1 for X, γ, e; W R = 20 for α particles).
  • SI Unit: Sievert (Sv) 1 Sv = 1 J/kg
  • Conventional Unit: Rem (Roentgen Equivalent Man) 1 Sv = 100 Rem

B. Effective Dose (E)

Accounts for the varying sensitivity of different tissues and organs to radiation-induced stochastic (long-term) effects.

E = ΣT (HT × WT)
  • Definition: Sum of the equivalent doses (HT) to all organs, each weighted by the Tissue Weighting Factor (WT), which reflects the organ's sensitivity to stochastic effects (e.g., bone marrow has a high WT).
  • Unit: Sievert (Sv). This unit is used for radiation protection and setting dose limits to ensure the overall risk is standardized regardless of the exposure pattern.
