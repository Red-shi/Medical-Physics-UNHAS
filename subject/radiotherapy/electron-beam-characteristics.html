Targeting shallow lesions with high precision using charged particles.

Electron beams are charged particles used primarily to treat shallow-seated tumors (e.g., skin, chest wall, lymph nodes) and boost the dose to superficial tumor bed regions. Their key characteristic is their ability to deposit a uniform dose up to a certain depth and then exhibit a rapid, sharp dose fall-off, effectively sparing underlying critical structures.

Electron Beam Production and Shaping

  • Production: In a Linac, the electron gun and accelerating waveguide are used, but the beam is not directed at a high-Z target. Instead, it bypasses the X-ray target and flattening filter.
  • Scattering Foil: The narrow electron beam must be widened to a clinically useful field size using a thin metal foil (scattering foil) placed in the beam path.
  • Collimation and Cones: Electron beams are strongly influenced by scattering in air and collimating material. Dedicated electron applicators (cones) are attached to the Linac head to define the field size and ensure a fixed distance to the patient (Source-to-Surface Distance - SSD).
  • Field Shaping: Unlike photons that use Multi-Leaf Collimators (MLC) near the source, electron beams are shaped using custom-cut cerrobend blocks placed near the patient surface to minimize the penumbra effect caused by scattering.

Primary Characteristic: Depth Dose Distribution

The Percent Depth Dose (PDD) curve for electrons defines their clinical utility, which is fundamentally different from photons.

1. The Electron PDD Curve

The electron PDD curve is characterized by three main regions:

  1. Surface Dose: Typically higher than MV photons (up to 80-95% for lower energies) because the electrons are already charged particles and begin depositing energy immediately upon entering the skin.
  2. Dose Plateau (Therapeutic Range, R80): A relatively flat region where the dose is close to maximum, suitable for treating the tumor.
  3. Rapid Fall-off (Practical Range, Rp): A sharp drop to zero dose, providing the definitive "cut-off" depth. This is the main advantage over photons.

2. Range Parameters

  • Depth of Maximum Dose (Dmax): Occurs at a shallow depth (typically 0.5 to 2.0 cm) and is generally independent of field size.
  • Practical Range, (Rp): The depth at which the dose drops to zero. This value is used to estimate the maximum penetration depth.
  • Therapeutic Range (R90 or R80): The depth to which 90% or 80% of the maximum dose is delivered. R80 is commonly used to approximate the useful treatment depth.
  • Penetration Estimation: The approximate therapeutic range (R80) and practical range (Rp) are directly related to the nominal electron energy (E in MeV):
    R80 ≈ E/3 cm   |   Rp ≈ E/2 cm
    *(Example: A 9 MeV beam has a useful range of approximately 3 cm.)*

Physics of Electron Interaction and Scatter

Electron interactions are governed by Coulomb forces (repulsion/attraction), leading to significant scattering.

  • Direct Ionization: As charged particles, electrons directly ionize atoms and excite molecules.
  • Bremsstrahlung Contamination: Although electrons are the intended particles, some electrons interact with the collimators or scattering foil, producing unwanted low-level X-ray (photon) contamination. This contamination contributes to the small, non-zero dose deep beyond the Rp.
  • Multiple Coulomb Scattering: Electrons undergo numerous small-angle deflections as they traverse the medium. This effect causes the beam to spread out, resulting in the characteristic wide penumbra at depth.
  • Heterogeneity Effects: Electron dosimetry is highly sensitive to inhomogeneities:
    • Air Cavities (e.g., Lung): Dose decreases distal to the air cavity.
    • Bone/High Density Tissue: Dose decreases within the dense tissue but can increase distal to the bone due to the reduced penetration and subsequent scatter.

Clinical Limitation: Due to significant scattering, electron beams lose their sharp dose fall-off and uniformity when treating curved surfaces or highly irregular tissue geometries. This requires careful planning and bolus application.
