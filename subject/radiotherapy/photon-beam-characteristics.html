Understanding the properties of Megavoltage X-ray beams for clinical dosimetry.

In External Beam Radiotherapy (EBRT), Megavoltage (MV) X-rays (photons) are the workhorse. The clinical use and accurate dosimetry of these beams require a thorough understanding of their physical characteristics. These characteristics are measured by medical physicists during the commissioning of a Linear Accelerator (Linac) and are essential inputs for the Treatment Planning System (TPS).

Primary Characteristics: Depth Dose Distribution

The distribution of dose as a function of depth in the patient is the single most important characteristic of a photon beam.

1. Percent Depth Dose (PDD)

  • Definition: PDD is the ratio (expressed as a percentage) of the absorbed dose at any specific depth (d) to the absorbed dose at a fixed reference depth (usually the depth of maximum dose, Dmax ), along the central axis of the beam.
  • Formula:
    PDD(d) = (Dose at depth d) / (Dose at Dmax) × 100%
  • Dependence: PDD increases with increasing beam energy (higher MV), increasing field size, and increasing Source-to-Surface Distance (SSD).

2. Tissue Maximum Ratio (TMR) / Tissue Phantom Ratio (TPR)

TMR (and its modern equivalent, TPR) is used for dose calculations when the machine's rotation center (Isocenter) is the reference point (Isocentric Technique).

Definition: TMR is the ratio of the absorbed dose at a depth d in the phantom to the absorbed dose at the depth of Dmax, both measured at the same distance from the source (Isocenter). Unlike PDD, TMR is essentially independent of the SSD, simplifying calculations for rotational therapy (VMAT, IMRT).

Secondary Characteristics: Lateral Beam Properties

The profile of the dose across the beam defines how well the beam shape conforms to the target volume.

3. Beam Profile

  • Definition: A plot of the absorbed dose across the beam, perpendicular to the central axis, at a specific depth.
  • Regions of the Profile:
    • Flatness: The uniformity of the dose in the central area of the beam (typically defined within 80% of the field width). Modern Linacs use a **flattening filter** to achieve a uniform dose profile.
    • Symmetry: The dose distribution on one side of the central axis should mirror the other side. Asymmetry indicates a problem with the Linac's beam steering.
    • Penumbra: The fuzzy region at the edge of the beam where the dose drops rapidly (usually defined between the 80% and 20% isodose lines). A sharp penumbra is desirable for conformal therapy.

4. Output Factor (Output Measurement)

The Output Factor Scp is a measure of the machine's ability to deliver dose. It links the monitor units (MU) set by the TPS to the actual dose delivered to the patient.

Total Output Factor is the product of two factors:

  1. Sc (Collimator Scatter Factor): Accounts for changes in scatter radiation produced by the machine's head (collimator and jaws) as the field size changes.
  2. Sp (Phantom Scatter Factor): Accounts for changes in the dose due to scatter radiation produced within the patient/phantom as the field size changes.

Photon Beam Quality and Energy

The "quality" or effective energy of an MV beam is determined by the accelerating potential of the Linac (e.g., 6 MV, 10 MV, 18 MV) and is characterized by its ability to penetrate tissue.

  • Nominal Energy: The peak energy of the electrons striking the target (e.g., 6 MeV electrons producing 6 MV X-rays).
  • Half-Value Layer (HVL): While mainly used for low-energy X-rays, the TMR/TPR or the depth of maximum dose (Dmax) are more commonly used in MV radiotherapy to specify beam quality. Higher energy means deeper penetration and higher Dmax.
  • Flattening Filter Free (FFF) Beams: Some modern Linacs remove the flattening filter. This results in:
    • Higher dose rate (faster treatment).
    • Conical beam profile (forward-peaked).
    • Reduced head scatter and softer energy spectrum.
