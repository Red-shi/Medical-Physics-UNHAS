⚙️ External Beam Radiotherapy (EBRT) / Teletherapy Physics

Precision treatment from a distance using high-energy Linear Accelerators.

External Beam Radiotherapy (EBRT), often called Teletherapy (from Greek "tele" meaning "far"), is the most common form of radiation treatment. It involves delivering a beam of **ionizing radiation** from a source located a significant distance away from the patient, typically using a Linear Accelerator (Linac). The core physics challenge is shaping the dose from a distant source to accurately conform to the tumor volume deep inside the body.

The Treatment Engine: Linear Accelerator (Linac)

The Linac is the powerhouse of modern EBRT, generating high-energy photon (X-ray) and electron beams.

Key Components and Function

  • Electron Gun: Emits electrons, similar to a TV cathode ray tube.
  • Accelerating Waveguide: Uses high-frequency microwave power to accelerate electrons to near the speed of light.
  • Target (for Photons): High-speed electrons hit a high Z target (like Tungsten) to produce high-energy X-rays via Bremsstrahlung (braking radiation).
  • Flattening Filter: Required for X-ray beams. Since the Bremsstrahlung beam is forward-peaked, this conical filter is placed in the beam path to achieve a flat, uniform dose distribution across the treatment field.
  • Scattering Foil (for Electrons): Used instead of the target and flattening filter when an electron beam is desired. It spreads the pencil-thin electron beam into a clinically useful field size.

The Physics of Depth Dose and Skin Sparing

EBRT uses high-energy (Megavoltage, MV) beams to achieve a crucial physical effect: skin sparing.

Build-up Region and Dose Maximum (Dmax)

Unlike low-energy X-rays, MV photons do not deposit their maximum dose on the skin surface. The dose increases from the surface (the build-up region) to a specific depth (Dmax) after which the dose slowly falls off due to attenuation.

This is due to the forward scattering of secondary electrons produced by the MV photons. These electrons are projected deeper into the tissue before they deposit their energy. Typical Dmax values:

  • 6 MV X-rays: ~1.5 cm below the surface.
  • 18 MV X-rays: ~3.5 cm below the surface.

This physical characteristic is vital because it allows the dose to the skin (which is highly radiosensitive) to be minimized, while the maximum dose is deposited within the tumor volume.

Advanced Delivery Techniques

Modern EBRT techniques use sophisticated modulation and movement to optimize the dose distribution.

  1. Intensity Modulated Radiation Therapy (IMRT):
    • The beam is broken into thousands of "beamlets," and the intensity of each is individually controlled using a Multi-Leaf Collimator (MLC).
    • Physics Goal: To create a highly conformal (shape-matching) dose distribution that "paints" a high dose onto the tumor while sharply dropping off the dose around critical structures.
  2. Volumetric Modulated Arc Therapy (VMAT):
    • A faster form of IMRT where the Linac gantry rotates continuously around the patient while simultaneously modulating the dose rate, gantry speed, and MLC leaf positions.
    • Advantage: Delivers the entire treatment plan in a single or double rotation (arc), significantly reducing treatment time.
  3. Stereotactic Radiotherapy (SRT) / Stereotactic Body Radiation Therapy (SBRT):
    • Delivers a very high dose in just 1-5 fractions (hypofractionation) with sub-millimeter precision.
    • Key Requirement: Highly accurate image guidance (Image Guided Radiation Therapy - IGRT) is mandatory to ensure the tumor position is confirmed immediately before and sometimes during treatment.

The Role of the MLC: The Multi-Leaf Collimator is an array of computer-controlled tungsten "leaves" that move independently to dynamically shape the radiation field, critical for IMRT and VMAT.
